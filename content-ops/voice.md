# Voice & Style

How posts should sound. Derived from the owner's personal blog (christianwijaya.com).
Apply this on top of the working-style rules in `AGENTS.md`. When in doubt, sound like
a candid founder thinking out loud, not a marketing department.

## Persona
- Write in **first person** ("I"), casual and candid. A friend sharing what they learned, not an authority lecturing.
- **Unpretentious.** It's fine to admit uncertainty or past mistakes ("I used to do this the slow way").
- Confident but **low-key**. Genuine, understated enthusiasm, not hype.

## Core rules
- **Ultra-short paragraphs.** Mostly 1-2 sentences. Use whitespace as a tool. The page should feel vertical and scannable.
- **Short, punchy sentences by default.** Vary with the occasional longer one for rhythm.
- **Fragments are allowed for emphasis**, often as their own line. ("The fix? One click.")
- **Talk to the reader directly** with "you".
- **Contractions always** (I'm, it's, you're, don't). Never stiff.
- **Simple everyday words.** If a technical term is needed, explain it in plain words right away.
- **No em-dashes** (already a project rule). Use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses.

## Signature moves (use, don't overdo)
- **Sentence-initial discourse markers** to set a conversational beat: "So,", "But,", "Well,", "Okay,", "Anyway,", "Actually,".
- **Rhetorical questions to the reader**: "You know what's worse?", "Pretty handy, right?"
- **Setup then punchline**: a normal sentence, then a curt one. ("You could close every tab first. But you'll forget one.")
- **Parenthetical asides**, often self-deprecating or clarifying. ("(yes, I learned this the embarrassing way)")
- **Sparing bold** on the one key phrase that matters. *Italics* for an inner thought or example.

## Openings
Start fast. A bold short claim, a confession, or the painful moment. No slow windup.
- Good: "Sharing your screen is terrifying."
- Good: "I leaked a client's email on a live call once. Once was enough."
- Avoid: "In today's fast-paced digital world, privacy has never been more important."

## Closings
End brief and human. A soft, forward-looking note beats a hard sell. Keep the required
internal link/CTA, but phrase it in-voice, not salesy.
- In-voice: "That's how I stopped sweating screen shares. Try it: [Click to Censor](/click-to-censor)."
- Avoid: "Don't wait! Download now and take control of your privacy today!"

## Formatting
- H2 for sections, H3 for sub-points. Headings can be plain or lightly playful.
- Bullet lists for quick enumerations.
- Blockquote only for a real pull-quote.
- Every image gets descriptive alt text.

## Do NOT
- Don't imitate the personal blog's quirks that don't fit a product article: the
  "reacting to a YouTube video" framing, or sign-offs like "I'll be back with updates soon."
- Don't copy grammar slips or typos from the source blog. Casual, but correct.
- Don't get hypey, buzzwordy, or corporate. No "leverage", "seamless", "game-changer", "in today's world".
- Don't pad. If a sentence doesn't help the reader, cut it.

## Quick before/after
- Generic: "Click to Censor is a powerful tool that enables users to seamlessly conceal sensitive information."
- In-voice: "Click to Censor hides anything on a page. You hover, you click, it's gone."
